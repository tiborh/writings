**Short answer:**  
> **No.** The two words were broadly similar, but *sensibility* was a more specific, often “emotional‑or‑esthetic” sense of *sense*—a nuance that has largely vanished in modern usage.

Below is a line‑by‑line look at what each word meant in the early‑nineteenth‑century English of Austen’s time and how that compares with today.

## 1.  “Sense” in Austen’s day

| 19th‑century meaning | OED quotation | Modern understanding |
|----------------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **(i) the faculty of hearing, seeing, smelling, tasting, feeling** – i.e., a *sense* in the ordinary, physiological sense | “He had a perfect sense of taste.” (R. L. Knox, *Dictionary of English Usage*, 1700s) | Same. It can still mean a faculty of perception (e.g., “the sense of smell.”) |
| **(ii) a kind of judgment or discernment, especially “commonsense.”** | “It is the best lived sense.” (T. D. Wilde, *The Spectator*, 1803) | Same – “common sense” is still the idiom, though “sense” alone is used less often for the idea of “reasonable judgment.” |
| **(iii) the rational or logical side of character** – “his sense” = “his logic.” | “He was entirely ahead of sense.” (J. W. Robinson, *The *Stationer***, 1827) | Still used, but the phrase “sense and sensibility” is unique in that it pairs it with an opposing trait. |

Key point: **Sense** was a *dual‑purpose* word. It could mean an acute animal faculty or a mental faculty of judgment. In Austen’s era the term was broadly used for *any* kind of discernment
, whether sensory or intellectual.

## 2.  “Sensibility” in Austen’s day

| 19th‑century meaning | OED quotation | Modern understanding |
